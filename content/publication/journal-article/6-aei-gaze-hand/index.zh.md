abstract: Consumer-grade eye trackers often suffer from low precision, limiting the reliability of gaze-based interaction. This paper explores a coordination-oriented design space for gaze-hand cascaded interaction under low-precision conditions. We systematically investigate three core dimensions—Keyboard Interaction Mode (Direct vs. Grouped), Gaze-Hand Switching Mode (Explicit vs. Implicit), and Gaze-Touch Confirmation Mode (Press vs. Align)—yielding eight concrete text entry methods. Through a mixed-method study on a commercial desktop eye tracker, we compare these methods quantitatively and qualitatively, and derive three actionable design principles for building robust gaze-hand systems in the presence of gaze offset and tracking noise.
